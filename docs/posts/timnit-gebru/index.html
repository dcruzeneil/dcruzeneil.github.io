Introduction

Dr. Timnit Gebru, a prominent voice in the field of artificial intelligence and computer vision is visiting Middlebury College virtually on the 24th of April. Here Dr. Gebru will talk about “Eugenics and the Promise of Utopia through Artificial General Intelligence”. Dr. Gebru is known for her work in algorithmic bias and the ethical implications of AI. She cofounded the “Black in AI” group and co-authored the paper “Gender Shades”, which exposed racial and gender bias in facial recognition software.

Dr. Timnit Gebru’s Talk on Computer Vision

In this talk, Dr. Timnit Gebru talks about the different nuances of the intersectionality of race and computer vision. She begins her argument by making it clear that computer vision is harming certain marginalized communities, for example the black community, and it is these very communities which have extremely low representation in the field, exacerbating the problem even further. She divides the ongoing problem with computer vision into two parts:
  • Representation in the Data-Set
  • Representation beyond Data-Set

Representation in the Data-Set

Through numerous examples, Dr. Gebru highlights how certain applications of computer vision have questionable impacts on people of certain communities. She starts off by talking about FACECEPTION a startup which predicts your “personality” or “group” based on your facial structure. After visiting their website, I found out that their “classification” could be something as trivial as “Professional Poker Player” to something as serious as “terrorist”. This system of recognizing “terrorists” based on facial structure, will ignore everything else, including but not limited to government-funded terrorism, and will amplify systematic racism - with certain marginalized groups being more likely to be labelled as “terrorists”. She further talks about a startup called HireVue and links it to the automation bias. HireVue analyzes your face from during an interview to try to detect your “internal state”. There is no proof if this method adheres to Equal Employment Opportunity standard, and the flaws of the system combined with automation bias - blindly trusting this system, when it is wrong - would lead to some people being constantly rejected from jobs.

She talks about how a lot of these problems stem from datasets not being diverse. Furthermore, she highlights how this problem is not just specific to computer vision, but also manifests itself in areas like “air-bag” testing for cars, and drug-testing on humans to name a few. This is because a lot of the publicly available dataset is of lighter shaded men and have very few darker shaded women. Furthermore, through an example of a classifier which classifies whether a given picture is of a wedding or not, she highlights how the model’s idea of a wedding was very heteronormative and western. Most of the times these computer vision applications are invasive, like the one used by Baltimore police, therefore the idea of having more diverse datasets is definitely an important one. But is that enough?

Representation beyond Data-Set

Apart from the need of representation in the dataset, we must also talk about “structural representation” - having people who share your interests. The reason why the computer vision community needs structural representation is because there are systems being developed and employed, which have a negative impact on certain communities, and more often than not, these communities are not part of the decision making process. For example, there was a system being developed - which we also discussed in class - which assign or predict the gender of the person based on their facial structures. First of all, one can imagine how the very assumption that a system can predict someone’s gender based on their facial structure is deep-rooted in stereotypical notions of “gender”. Combine that with the fact that sometimes this face data is obtained unethically, and lastly combine that with question: why do we need a system which can predict people’s gender? It is harmful and puts the transgender community in danger, especially in conservative countries, and the only people it is potentially helping is multi-national corporation, for their targeted advertising. And even this targeted advertising would further gender-stereotypes, as the very implication of gender-targeted advertising is that you have a box of things that you think people of a specific gender do and enjoy. She also talks about imperfect systems combined with imperfect use aggravates bias. She talked about how not too long ago, people were not expected to disclose how a certain model worked and what was the error rate. And furthermore, sometimes people did not even use a model (which is imperfect to begin with) the way it was intended to be used. For instance, people would use sketches and photos of look-alike actors/actresses in models which explicitly need the photo of the suspect and this would lead to imperfect accusations, or at times arrests.

One thing which really stuck out to me was the parallel she made from during the WW-II, where she presented a newspaper article which highlighted how to tell apart “Japanese Americans” from “Chinese Americans”. She linked this to our current applications of computer vision, and talked about if this is really the direction we want to go in.

Too Long; Didn’t Read (TL;DR)

The field of computer vision lacks both structural representation and representation in datasets, which puts certain communities at risk of negatively being affected by the applications of computer vision. Furthermore, these are the communities which are not involved in any of the decision-making processes which further exacerbates the issue.

Question for Dr. Timnit Gebru

There is a heavy focus on representation in the dataset when it comes to dimnishing the biases present in these computer vision models. However, from your talk at the FATE/CV 2020, it also seems that everytime there is an attempt to represent a marginalized community in the dataset, it usually has several negative implications. For instance, facial data of darker skinned people being unethically mined, and face data of transgender people putting them at risk especially in conservative countries. What is the best way to go about this representation which will positively affect these communities?

Dr. Timnit Gebru’s Talk at Middlebury College

On the 24th of April, I got the opportunity to attend Dr. Gebru’s talk on “Eugenics and the Promise of Utopia through Artificial General Intelligence”. In this talk she explored the intricate relationship between eugenics and the concept of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). Furthermore, she raised important questions regarding the “utopia” that certain proponents of AGI promise and made us question what exactly this utopia is, and who it would benefit.

Dr. Gebru’s initial focus was on the TESCREAL ideologies (Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, and Longtermism). She argued how these ideologies, which are often adopted by propoents of AGI, are related to the ideologies which were adopted across different waves of eugenics. By giving examples of the type of literature written by certain transhumanists, she mainly raised the following arguments:
  1. What exactly is AGI and why do we need it?
  2. How the TESCREAL ideologies of “utopia” and “apocalypse” are linked to eugenics
  3. How the AI apocalypse is prevalent, but in the way one might imagine

What exactly is AGI and why do we need it?

Dr. Gebru started off by raising an important question regarding the inherent nature of AGI. By showing us the literature surrounding AGI and its proponents, she came to the conclusion that most of these people talk about AGI as some sort of “god-like” system, without having a clear idea of what it is exactly they are trying to create. According to her, our focus should be working on “well-defined systems” which are designed for a specific problem.

How the TESCREAL ideologies of “utopia” and “apocalypse” are linked to Eugenics

Dr. Gebru described the TESCREAL ideologies and how that is linked to eugenics. Transhumanism was an example that she addressed, and spoke about how the very idea of defining an “enhanced” human introduces a hierarchical structure of which human features are “desirable” and “better”. This clearly follows a similar pattern of thoughts that were adopted by eugenicists. Furthermore to solidify her arguments, she talked about how both eugenics and the TESCREAL ideologies talk about a “utopia” and an “apocalypse”. She related this to AGI by talking about how its proponents like to argue that AGI will be able to solve all of the world’s problems, alluding to this alleged “utopia”. Therefore, establishing the relationship between AGI and the TESCREAL ideologies.

How the apocalypse is prevalent, but not in the way one might imagine

Dr. Gebru claimed that most of the times when people talk about the AI (or AGI) apocalypse, they make it seem sci-fi, but that takes the attention away from the actual apocalypse that manifests itself in various ways on a daily basis. She talked about the dangers of Large Language Models (LLM)s and how they perpetuate discriminatory views across class, gender, and race. Furthermore, she talked about how this increased attention to AGI is inevitably feeding into the capitalistic system. In a situation where companies manage to create these “god-like” systems, that would heavily increase our dependence on these companies which would widen the gap between the rich and the poor, and isolate a lot of power in the hands of these companies. Therefore, we would be in a situation where these people in power will get to revolutionize society in ways they deem appropriate. She gave examples of how these companies exploit people by underpaying moderators. Furthermore, these moderators have to review content generated by AGI, in some cases these could be things which are toxic and could affect the moderators’ mental health. There are also issues regarding how the data is obtained to train these models, and whether the data is ethically mined or not. All in all, she claimed that there are a lot of issues with the way these companies function, and there are also a lot of prevalent issues in society which need more urgent attention. She gave an example by talking about global food insecurity, and by questioning how exactly AGI will solve these issues and achieve its “utopia”.

My Perspectives

Overall, I agree with what Dr. Gebru said. By chasing developments in AGI, these big companies are chasing goals which they themselves are not completely sure of. Furthermore, this will definitely lead to concentration of power in the hands of few. After listening to her talk, I have some questions regarding some of the things she spoke about:
  1. This concentration of power will definitely happen, but does that not manifest itself even in the present world (think about big tech companies). Will the advent of AGI further this concentration because companies would try to come up with a system that “solves” all issues and can do anything for you, therefore increasing your reliance on a single company? But, if we take a company let us say Google, are we not on that stage already where we are heavily reliant on a single company in our daily lives?
  2. If the way the LLMs are trained make them prone to perpetuate discriminatory views, how can we change the way they are trained or the data they are trained on to account for these biases?

Reflection on the Process

Overall, this talk was an eye-opening experience for me. Through this talk I was able to understand the relationship between AGI and eugenics, something which I had not thought about before. Furthermore, through her examples, she highlighted the exploitative nature of this industry, and the way the workers are treated is just a reflection of the capitalistic cycle we would be getting into by allowing this isolation of power. Personally, I had always consumed media which talked about the AI apocalypse as this doomsday where machines will take over, but it was a reality check to understand the true nature of the “apocalypse”, and how certain groups are already being harmed by these developments. Having studied biases prevalent in machine learning models for this class, it was interesting to hear someone from the industry talk about how urgent these issues are, and the scales at which they affect people. I am very interested in understanding how we can advance AI in ways which benefit everyone and does not cause harm to any group, and this is something that I would love to explore further.
